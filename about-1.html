Mayor Martin J. Walsh Boston Transportation Department

Go Boston 2030 is a City of Boston initiative to envision a bold transportation future for Boston for the next 5, 10 and 15 years.

The plan will develop a far-reaching vision that proposes transformative polices and projects to improve transportation for the city’s residents, businesses and visitors. The 18-month process is being driven by data and steered through an unprecedented and inclusive public engagement process. Go Boston 2030 will be developed with a Vision Report including goals and targets released in spring 2015 and an Action Plan specifying policies and projects finalized in early 2016.

What can Boston city government do — whether in partnership with others or by itself — to increase mobility while preserving and improving the environment, economy and livability?

- Mayor Martin J. Walsh

This plan begins from the premise that it’s about people and how they get around. The plan will propose how transportation can support significant improvements in

Environment

The plan will work to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from transportation by reducing the number of miles driven per person and increasing the number of trips made without a car. Transportation infrastructure should be prepared for extreme weather events.

Economy

The plan will foster connections to employment, support development and neighborhood revitalization, and enhance growth of new industries like healthcare and technology start-ups.

Equity

The plan will strive for all Bostonians to have access to quality transportation that helps them reach jobs, education, health care, affordable housing, healthy food, culture and open space opportunities. Transportation options in every neighborhood should be accessible and safe.

Timeline

Driving Directions
2000

In 2000, people were excited to print driving directions from websites like MapQuest instead of relying on traditional maps. Today, many people use their smart phones to provide directions for multiple modes of transportation instantaneously.

T Tokens, Cards, and Tickets
2003

In 2000, many T riders still relied on T tokens and paper tickets. Today, the Charlie Card allows riders to easily store-value, auto-renew monthly passes, and tap their cards at the entry gates without removing it from a wallet.

Car Share
200X

Car share started in Boston in 2000 when Zipcar opened for business in June. The concept has now expanded across the country eliminating the need to own a private vehicle for thousands of urban dwellers. Today, different business models are testing one-way and electric sharing systems.

Bike Program
200X

In 2000, there were roughly 50 miles of paths and greenways but there were virtually no on-street bike lanes. Today, there are more than 90 miles of bike lanes in addition to shared lane markings, bike boxes, and cycle tracks. Since 2007, the City distributed more than 3,500 bicycles through “Roll It Forward” and teaches 5,000 Boston public school students how to safely ride a bike each year.

Greenways
200X

Boston had not built a greenway in decades when the East Boston Greenway opened in 2007. It now provides paths for walking, running, and cycling through a linear park. Other linear parks, like the Neponset River Greenway, have also been built in the past 15 years.

MBTA Communication
200X

Today, 314 countdown clocks at 53 stations provide MBTA riders with information about when their next train will arrive. By sharing data about the location of the trains and buses with app developers, the MBTA helps smart phone users get up-to-the minute information about the arrival of their next bus.

Bike Share
200X

Hubway launched in 2011 and expanded into Cambridge, Somerville, and Brookline in 2012. The bike share program now has over 1,300 active members and has logged more than 2 million rides. One-day, three-day, monthly, and annual memberships are available, and low-income Boston residents are eligible for subsidized memberships.

Open Streets
200X

Six “Circle the City” events have been held in collaboration with the City of Boston since 2012. By closing streets to cars and opening them to people on Sunday afternoons, these events build community and allow people to look at their neighborhoods in a new way.

Complete Streets
200X

Boston’s Complete Streets guidelines were published in 2013 and are consistently implemented across the city in order to support multimodal, green, and smart design street reconstruction. The designs support users of all ages and abilities, incorporate healthy trees and plants, and are equipped to facilitate multiple modes and transfers between them.

Parklets
200X

Parklets – public spaces that fill up the space of one or two parking spaces commercial areas with limited open space – have been installed in Roslindale, Allston, Jamaica Plain, and Mission Hill in 2013 and 2014. This is one of the many examples of the way the city has come to view the street as a shared space for people.

Ride Share
200X

The surge of new businesses trying to offer taxi-like services hailed from smart phones demonstrate that how we get around in other people’s cars is rapidly changing. A start-up that offers crowd-sourced bus services is another example of how communication technology is transforming transportation.
